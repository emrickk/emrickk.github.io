Original: Top 10 Outdoor Survival Tools\
Translated version: Top 10 Outdoor Survival Tools\
Translated by: Cheng

Most outdoor enthusiasts fret over what to pack in an outdoor emergency kit. We consider these the must-have supplies for crossing desert wilderness. A GPS and a satellite phone would of course be enormously useful, but this collection is mostly the cheap backups for when those things get damaged or stop working.\
10. High-calorie protein items\
Hunting and fishing cost more than they return. Chasing down food burns more calories than you gain from eating the meat of the catch. Carrying some snacks will spare your energy.\
9. Flashlight\
A flashlight can be spotted by search-and-rescue teams from far away. It's a good way to draw attention to yourself, and a very handy method when you can't light a signal fire.

8. Whistle\
When you're lost in the wild, the first thing is to send a signal for help. Blowing a whistle at intervals uses less energy than shouting, and the sound carries farther.\
7. Tent\
In brutally hot or brutally cold conditions, being able to shield yourself from harm is half of a successful trip. You can always take it apart and build a more refined shelter.\
6. Water purification gear\
Drinking water heavy with salts harms your health. With proper filtering and the removal of contaminants, you can spare yourself a lot more trouble.\
5. Plastic garbage bags\
Thin and light, they're worth far more than they weigh, so make full use of them. Use them against the rain, cut three holes for an improvised poncho, and they'll block the wind too. Bring several along. Garbage bags are the sturdiest kind.\
4. First aid kit\
First aid matters even more than being lost. Your kit should carry supplies for handling serious injuries-not just cuts and scrapes. Bring some Quick Clot or Celox for heavy bleeding, then add tweezers, needle and thread, antibiotics, sunscreen, and any special medications you need, ready for emergencies.\
3. Metal cup or container\
Staying hydrated matters far more than finding food, and with a metal container you can sterilize at high heat and kill the microbes.\
2. A light, easy-burning fuel canister\
Starting a fire with a flint is easy in dry weather, but in the damp you may need a lighter and some tinder to get a flame going. Cotton balls coated in wax, a purpose-made backpacking stove burner, or a bottle of Bacardi 151 are all good choices.\
1. Knife\
Never leave it at home. A sharp knife is essential for building shelter, preparing food, getting the campfire evening going, and handling everything else that comes up.
