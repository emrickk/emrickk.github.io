title: 'What You Gave Up Is Worth the Most'
description: 'A repost of an essay by Amy Cheung on why what you cannot have, and what you gave up, always seems best.'
---

That person was always good to you, yet you never fell in love with him.\
You told yourself that someone with your qualities really had no need to settle.\
Then one day he began a romance with someone else, and to your own surprise your heart ached.\
Not only did it ache; worse, you discovered that you actually loved him.\
You were dying of jealousy toward his lover; you wanted him back at your side,\
yet you also wished to keep your dignity. The happier he is now, the more you suffer.\
So it turns out you already loved him very deeply.

The fish you fail to catch is always the biggest.

You do not love him as deeply as you suppose;\
you are only less confident than you suppose.\
Nor are the grapes you cannot reach sour; they are too sweet,\
so sweet you dare not imagine it, and can only call them sour.\
Some girls, on learning that an old love is seeing someone new, fall into utter dejection.\
She was the one who proposed the breakup, and she has a new boyfriend of her own,\
so why does she still feel that sour, vinegary twinge?

So it turns out the butterfly you set free is the most beautiful.\
The things you gave up are the most valuable.\
The puppy that wandered off is the most adorable.

I had a mongrel puppy that wandered away, and to this day I still think of it as clever and quick,\
lively and beautiful. In truth, I only vaguely remember how it looked.\
When your admirer's affections turn elsewhere, just tell yourself the puppy you kept has wandered off.\
Its virtue lies precisely in being gone.

What you have not yet obtained is worth a hundred points; once it is in hand, only 59 remain
