On our first morning in Amsterdam the sky was gray. The hotel window came in two pages: on the left, a row of old houses packed shoulder to shoulder; on the right, a clock tower in the distance. No two houses were the same height, and each wore a different hat. Together they looked like a family portrait.

![The hotel room's double window over Amsterdam's rooftops](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/amsterdam-hotel-window-room.webp)

The next afternoon we slipped into the Nine Streets, a little net of lanes woven between the canals, shops standing one against the next. The head of a bridge is the best seat in the neighborhood: a couple propped their bike against the railing and leaned there, watching the street. We stood behind them and watched them watch the street. Nobody was in a hurry to leave.

![A couple leaning on the railing at a bridge in the Nine Streets](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/amsterdam-nine-streets-couple.webp)

We turned into a coffee shop to rest our feet, small and busy. The door stood open, and over it ran a band of stained glass, orange and blue squares set in a white grid. It took coming to the Netherlands for it to click: Mondrian may have learned his grids from doorways like this one.

![Looking out the open door of the coffee shop, with stained glass above it](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/amsterdam-cheese-shop-door.webp)

In the evening we boarded a canal boat, built for one thing only: the roof is glass, and so are the walls. On a shelf sat a single wooden clog. Decoration, or the captain's spare shoe, hard to say.

The boat headed out into the IJ first. Across the water a snow-white building crouched at the waterline: the film museum. The name takes no detours: the river is called the IJ, which sounds about like "eye," so the museum is called EYE.

![The glass-roofed cabin of the canal boat, with the EYE museum across the water](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/amsterdam-glass-boat-eye.webp)

Another glass boat came the other way, packed to the last seat. We looked at them through two layers of glass, and they looked back at us: two fish tanks meeting in traffic.

![Another tour boat passing, seen through the boat window](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/amsterdam-passing-glass-boat.webp)

The boat turned back into the old town, and a tower leaned out from between the window frame and its reflections, brick body, white crown. It is called the Montelbaanstoren. Four hundred years ago, when the clock on top was new, it rang whenever it pleased, midnight included, and the people of Amsterdam nicknamed it Malle Jaap, Crazy Jaap. It did not make a sound as we passed. Probably tired.

![The Montelbaanstoren between the window frame and reflections](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/amsterdam-montelbaanstoren.webp)

Somewhere along the way the clouds mostly gave up and the light turned gold. Houseboats lined both banks, and one hung a sign at the bow: a bed and breakfast that floats. Flower pots and wooden chairs stood on the deck, and a few people sat sunk in the chairs, taking the last of the sun.

All through Amsterdam I kept turning one thing over: why are the windows here so big? Houses have big windows, boats have big windows, the coffee shop gave its whole street-facing wall to glass, nothing like American houses with their small, careful windows. And yet the Dutch are famous for their boundaries: they say what they mean and keep the rules in plain sight. I thought about it the whole way back and only got halfway there: however big the glass, it never means "come in." It means "look all you want."

We had bought tickets to watch Amsterdam from behind glass; the houseboat owners pulled up chairs on their own bow and watched boatload after boatload of us go by. Nobody minded.
