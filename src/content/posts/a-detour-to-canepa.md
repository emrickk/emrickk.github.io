## Upstairs

We spent our first ten minutes with one small car: a Fiat Abarth, white with a red stripe, parked in the kind of direct afternoon sun that museums usually protect their exhibits from. Here it fell straight across the floor and nobody seemed worried.

![A white Fiat Abarth with a red stripe in afternoon window light](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/canepa-abarth-front.webp)

The room held race cars the way other rooms hold furniture: wood ceiling, white walls, eucalyptus in every window, and a placard at each set of wheels listing what the machine had won.

## Further in

The deeper we went, the stranger the cars and the longer the placards. Old Indianapolis roadsters with hand-lettered sponsor names. A row of racing Porsches whose histories read like service records. A motorcycle wearing a full teardrop fairing. Two dragsters. A machine with Porsche turbo painted on the side of what was unmistakably a tractor, which I am still thinking about.

## The shop floor

At the back, a railing. Below the railing, the part of the building that does not hold still: Gullwing Mercedes with their doors up, an orange 911 in mid-surgery, somebody's tires stacked and waiting. The museum upstairs is just the portion of this place that has been asked to be quiet for a while.

## The confession

None of this was planned. The day was supposed to be Santa Cruz: the Boardwalk, the beach, that was the whole itinerary. Then a building came up beside the road with too many cars around it to miss, so we turned in without knowing what it was. We looked it up afterwards: famous, apparently. That afternoon we may have been the only people in the building still finding out where we were.

## The showroom

Downstairs the placards turn into price lists. A white 918 Spyder holds the middle of the floor, a green 959 keeps to itself, a Ford GT waits in the dark end, and past them it is 911s in every direction, so many that after a while we stopped pointing them out to each other.

## Santa Cruz, eventually

Two hours later we were where we had been going all along. The Boardwalk in late October runs at half power: no lines, low sun, roasted corn on the air. We did not ride anything. We walked, and let the place carry on around us.
