One year ago, almost to the day, we were driving north out of San Francisco toward [the redwoods](/posts/two-days-in-the-redwoods/), and before the trees we finally kept a promise I had been making myself for years: ten minutes off the 101 in San Rafael, Frank Lloyd Wright's last commission, the Marin County Civic Center. I had known this building from photographs for a long time. The photographs, it turns out, undersell it.

Wright drew it in 1957, at ninety. He died in the spring of 1959, before ground was broken, and the county spent the sixties finishing it without him: two long wings riding from hilltop to hilltop, a sky-blue roof, a gold spire, and, in place of a ceiling, a skylight running the entire length of each wing. It is still a working government building. People come here to pay property taxes, file permits, get married, return library books. That is exactly what makes walking in so hard to believe.

![The gold anodized gates of the civic center lobby, a woman waiting on a bench in the sun](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/civic-center-gold-gates.webp)

## Corridors with sky in them

The building has almost no interior in the usual sense. The hallways are balconies, the balconies look down into gardens, and everything stands under that one long window of sky. Even the escalator ride is a small ceremony: you rise out of the shade straight toward the glass. None of it reads as sixty years old, which is presumably why, when Hollywood needed a ready-made future for Gattaca, it simply filmed here.

And it is pink. Nobody quite warns you about that. The walls are salmon, the floors are a red terrazzo polished until they hold the sky, and the window frames, gates and rails are all gold. On a July afternoon the fourth floor glowed like the inside of a seashell.

![The curved balcony corridor on the fourth floor, salmon walls glowing under the skylight](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/civic-center-curved-corridor.webp)

## The garden inside

Down the middle of every atrium runs a planter, and in the planters, not to put too fine a point on it, grows a jungle: schefflera, birds of paradise, philodendron, palms, all leaning toward the light. Half of my photographs from that afternoon are of architecture seen through leaves.

## The library

We ended the visit in the library at the top of the building: a round desk, a circular leather bench, red walls, the afternoon light coming down through the skylight and doing its best work. If nobody reminded you, you would not remember that this is, first of all, a county office.

![The library's round desk and circular bench under the skylight](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/civic-center-library-desk.webp)

What actually stayed with us is the condition of the place. The building opened in 1962 and looks like it opened last month: the terrazzo is polished, the gold anodizing is bright, the plants are thriving, the skylights are clean. For sixty-some years somebody has wiped it, repaired it, watered the plants, and left room for all of that in every year's budget. That was what we talked about for the rest of the drive north.

Wright never saw it built. We had a look on his behalf, then kept driving north.
