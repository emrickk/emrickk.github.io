One Saturday in July 2024, a week after the last downtown walk, we were back: errands in the city, finished early, a new camera that had never been outside, and roughly the same route, wandered more than planned. Looking back, the day had a second thread running through it, though it took the whole walk to see.

## City Lights

City Lights has kept the same argument going since 1953. Lawrence Ferlinghetti and Peter Martin opened it as the first all-paperback bookstore in the country; three years later Ferlinghetti published Allen Ginsberg's Howl, stood trial for obscenity over it, and won. To this day the banner in the window is hand-painted: BOOKS NOT BOMBS.

But the noisy world stays outside the glass, and inside it is quiet; in a seventy-year-old bookstore, the woman on the stool was reading her phone.

## Columbus Avenue

Columbus Tower has Coppola, who has looked after it since the seventies. The orange three-story down the way has nobody: it leans like the next strong wind could put it down, and it hasn't gone down. Downtown keeps its small survivors the only way it knows: surrounded.

## The prettiest corner

The white bank at 701 Montgomery, built in 1909 for John Fugazi, the first home of Transamerica, is still the prettiest thing on its block: arched windows, a cornice like a crown. The banks on this street each found a caretaker, a bookstore, a golf center; this one drew the Church of Scientology. A shame is about the size of it.

## What happens to banks

456 Montgomery is the orange building's other possible ending. Just as small, just as outgrown by its own street; but where that one leans and stays whole, this one got converted: the marble front kept like a specimen at the foot of the tower that replaced it, gold letters intact, an indoor golf center behind the brass doors. Standing perfectly straight, and a pity: only the face is left.

![The preserved bank front of 456 Montgomery Plaza at the foot of a tower](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/sf-456-montgomery.webp)

The colonnade at 400 California stands on the site of William Ralston's Bank of California. When the bank broke in August 1875, Ralston went for his usual swim in the bay and did not come back. At 465, behind the door with a gilded X, is the Merchants Exchange, where ship owners once watched the Gate for their arrivals, and where Julia Morgan kept her office for most of her career. Both names come up again.

Downtown San Francisco is past its best these days, and one of these halls stood open to show it: chandeliers lit, the counter curving around air, no business being done. Banks built themselves like temples so you would believe they could never close. The building, it turns out, is still keeping that promise; the bank isn't.

![An empty banking hall, chandeliers lit, sunlight on the marble floor](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/sf-empty-bank-lobby.webp)

## Market Street

Market Street keeps walking its old-timers: the F line runs mid-century PCC streetcars, collected from other cities, restored, on the regular schedule at full fare, not in a display case.

![A green F-line streetcar on Market Street, a woman crossing ahead of it](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/sf-market-streetcar.webp)

## One Montgomery, round two

One Montgomery is waiting for its next caretaker. Willis Polk's banking hall, landmarked, vacant since Wells Fargo left; last week it was simply empty, this week it had grown easels, big photographs of itself in its prime. Somebody is auditioning tenants, and the pitch is the building's own past.

## Post Street

Polk once more on the way over: the Hobart Building, said to be the one he called his best, with its name out over Market.

![The Hobart Building's name over Market Street, towers layered behind](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/sf-hobart-lettering.webp)

The most dutiful caretaker on the route is at 57 Post. The Mechanics' Institute has been lending books since 1854; Albert Pissis put up this building in 1910, and business has not stopped since. The country's oldest chess club is here too.

## The Palace

The Palace was Ralston's other monument; it opened five weeks after the bay took him. Over the bar, Maxfield Parrish's Pied Piper has hung since the hotel's 1909 reopening, and once, the caretaking fell to the whole city: in 2013 the owners tried to send the painting to auction in New York, and the objection was loud enough that they changed their minds, cleaned it, and hung it back. The children of Hamelin are still following; at the Market Street windows, the afternoon reduced everyone to silhouettes.

## Third and Market

The Hearst Building holds the corner of Third and Market, where the Examiner used to run. The entrance, tile and copper and coats of arms, is Julia Morgan's; her again. Inside it is empty too, waiting for a tenant like half the grand rooms on this walk.

## Another downtown

The balcony at home faces another downtown: San Jose, it turns out, is not a small city, just far quieter on weekdays.

![Downtown San Jose at dusk](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/sj-downtown-dusk.webp)

The two chairs on the balcony hold the best seat of the day. In the glass, the living room and the city sit in the same pane. Next time, a walk down there.
