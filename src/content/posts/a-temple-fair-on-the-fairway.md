One Sunday in the middle of August we drove two hours to Pebble Beach to watch a beauty pageant for cars. Its proper name is the Concours d'Elegance, French for "competition of elegance," held once a year on the 18th fairway of the golf course. The name sounds solemn. Walk in and it is a temple fair.

The pageant has one hard rule: pretty is not enough, the car has to run. When the judges are tied, the advantage goes to the car that finished Thursday's Tour d'Elegance, out along Highway One to Big Sur and back. And on Sunday afternoon the winners drive themselves across the fairway to collect their trophies. A good part of the field is past ninety.

## The way in

It starts in the parking lot: the cars the spectators drove here would make a decent show on their own. By the path, a kid in a bucket hat crouched down to set a stuffed puppy on the pavement, back turned to a row of Ferraris that nobody was watching.

A Mercedes 300 SL stood in the crowd. Through its side window, the ivory steering wheel and the queue outside stacked up in the same pane of glass.

## On the fairway

The field is sorted by brand and by decade. Ferrari took up a good stretch of it: SF90s and 296s parked in rows on the grass, and a toddler wobbled past the nose of one with an adult's hand trailing half a meter behind.

The fair had new stalls too. A green rebirth of the Lancia 037 parked in front of its own matching transporter, and on the concept lawn a brand-new machine called a Nilu held both doors up in the air, gold exhausts out in the open, a few people standing around it in no hurry at all.

The people out-dressed the cars: linen suits, floral dresses, more straw hats than car roofs. A woman in a bright yellow suit sat down on the running board of a prewar convertible to answer her messages, a small green bag standing at her feet. Along the seaward edge runs a low white fence, and outside the fence, the picnic: blankets, wicker hampers, wine, folding chairs. The sea starts right behind.

## The row by the water

The far end of the fairway leans on Stillwater Cove.

![The water of Stillwater Cove](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/stillwater-cove.webp)

The row closest to the water held the loud names: a red F40 facing a line of flags, and a few steps on, a teal Jaguar XJ220. Wedge-shaped concept cars were one of this year's featured classes, and the 1970 Pininfarina Modulo, white as a bar of soap, drew a crowd several rows deep.

Under the cypress stood a Duesenberg, its placard patiently spelling out the full name: 1931 Duesenberg J Murphy Convertible Coupe "Disappearing Top," which is longer than most resumes. At the picnic tables behind it, people ate lunch with their backs to it.

Packard turned 125 this year. A 1934 roadster held its door open toward the water, wearing a Virginia plate from the year it was built.

![A 1934 Packard roadster by the water, door open, wearing its 1934 Virginia plate](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/packard-by-the-cove.webp)

## The parade

Around three the awards began, and this is where the fair is most a fair: the winning cars are not pushed onto a stage. The owners drive them, straight through the crowd.

The queue waited at the edge of the lawn. A woman rested her arm on the window sill of an orange Lamborghini Miura, white-framed sunglasses, a watch over her wrist. When an orange coupe's turn came, smoke started pouring off its rear wheel and did not stop. In the back window sat a black French bulldog, tongue out.

Maserati's race cars were guests of honor this year. A file of red ones motored past the grandstand one by one; on the flank of one of them, lettered in white, the name of the man who once raced it: Carroll Shelby.

The car has to run. For the new ones that is nothing; for a ninety-year-old, it is already a small miracle. The news that evening said Best of Show went to a 1934 Bugatti Type 59, the first completely unrestored car to take the top prize in the event's seventy-three years. Original paint, original wear, and it runs.

## The way out

After four the crowd drained toward the gates, and we went the other way for one more pass along the water. The Maserati with the big 500 on its nose was still there between two blue flags, two people resting beside it. In front of the silver Bugatti EB110, two gentlemen in hats stood talking.

The exit runs through the manufacturers' stands, the fair's outer ring. Bugatti had the Tourbillon, released two months earlier, turning slowly on a turntable, complete with studio lights, potted plants, and a French flag.

![The Bugatti Tourbillon on a turntable, doors up](https://cdn.theneverless.com/2026/07/tourbillon-on-the-turntable.webp)

On the drive home, the thing that stayed was the first car we had passed on the way in: a CLK GTR, alone under a black canopy, a row of white sails hanging over it. All afternoon, every car out on the grass had to be able to run. It sat in the shade and never moved, and nobody asked it to.
